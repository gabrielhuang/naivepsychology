Revisiting the Id, Ego, and Superego

Freud's model

The id, ego, and superego model is a psychiatric model generally attributed to Sigmund Freud, which factorizes human behavior into an animal-like and impulsive part (id), a rational action taker (ego), and the need to satisfy external rules (superego).

Revisiting the id and ego

Here I present my own interpretation of these terms, which differ slightly from the usual definition because I think they are a more interesting factorization from a modeling persepective using the reinforcement-learning (RL) framework. Rather than distinguishing them based on impulsivity (id) vs. rationality (ego), I prefer to distinguish them as the reward function (id) vs. the agent which attempts to optimize such a reward (ego). Concerning the superego, my model does not consider it to be a separate agent in the brain. Rather, the superego is the process by which societal norms and expectations are internalized into our id and unconsciously tied to our emotions.

The id is our animal-like part or child-like part, and it's what distinguishes us from machines. It is the receptacle of all our emotions, including cravings and aversions, and is the driving force behind all our actions (under our model). The id:
- makes us feel alive.
- is the driving force behind *all* our actions
- generates our impulses, spontaneous desires, and emotions, but is not necessarily what causes them
- doesn't think in logical terms, and is more of a reactive nature.
- doesn't really have a notion of time like the ego, and cannot model the future.
- rather, the id remembers the past indirectly by generating various emotions depending on the situation.
- the id changes slowly, and is a product of our genetics and past experiences.
- the id be influenced, but only gradually, and over time.
